Research

Cooling and controlling individual atoms

I work in Dr. Jonathan Hood's lab, where we use tightly focused laser beams — optical tweezers — to trap single neutral atoms and study them in isolation. My work involves developing cooling techniques to bring atoms close to their motional ground state, and building toward experiments with mixtures of lithium and cesium atoms.

Lithium
Single ⁶Li atom in an optical tweezer
We developed a way to load a single lithium-6 atom directly from a magneto-optical trap into an optical tweezer, and used Λ-enhanced gray molasses cooling to image it with high fidelity over many repeated exposures. This work also led to a theoretical framework for optical cooling in tight traps.
Cesium
Narrow-line cooling of ¹³³Cs
We explored narrow-line quadrupole cooling of cesium atoms using the weak 6S₁/₂–5D₅/₂ transition, combined with vortex beams generated via a spatial light modulator. This allowed us to cool Cs atoms to a few microkelvin and image them with a suppressed background.
Future work
Toward ultracold LiCs molecules
The longer-term goal of the lab is to bring Li and Cs atoms together in shared tweezers, perform Feshbach spectroscopy, and eventually form ground-state LiCs molecules. The large mass imbalance between Li and Cs also makes sympathetic cooling an interesting possibility to explore.
