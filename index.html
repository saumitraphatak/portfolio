My doctoral work, submitted in May 2025, focuses on trapping and cooling individual lithium and cesium atoms in optical tweezers — tightly focused laser beams that can hold a single atom in place. Research

Cooling and imaging single atoms in optical tweezers

Optical tweezers — tightly focused laser beams — can trap a single neutral atom and hold it in isolation. Working with individual atoms this way lets us study quantum phenomena without averaging over large ensembles, but it also demands very precise control over the atom's internal and motional states. My thesis addresses some of the practical challenges involved in cooling these atoms and reading out their state with high reliability.

Lithium · Chapter 3
Cooling and imaging ⁶Li in an optical tweezer
Loading a single lithium-6 atom into a tweezer is challenging because ⁶Li is the lightest alkali, with a large recoil energy and unresolved hyperfine structure that makes standard cooling approaches less effective. We used Λ-enhanced gray molasses on the D1 line to simultaneously cool and image a single ⁶Li atom, achieving a survival probability of 0.9995 per image over ~2000 consecutive imaging cycles. Understanding why the cooling worked as well as it did led directly to the theoretical work in Chapter 2.
Theory · Chapter 2
A generalized framework for optical cooling in tight traps
To make sense of the experimental results with ⁶Li, we developed a 1D theoretical framework that treats sideband cooling, polarization gradient cooling, and gray molasses within a single master-equation formalism. The model revealed the relative efficiencies of different cooling strategies and showed that gray molasses can drive atoms close to their motional ground state in the Lamb-Dicke regime — a result that had not been worked out in detail before.
Cesium · Chapter 4
Narrow-line quadrupole cooling of ¹³³Cs
Cesium atoms can be cooled using the weak electric-quadrupole transition at 685 nm (6S₁/₂ → 5D₅/₂, linewidth ~118 kHz), which is narrow enough to resolve individual motional sidebands. We used a spatial light modulator to generate vortex beams with tailored orbital angular momentum for background-free imaging, and applied magic-ellipticity polarization to suppress differential light shifts. This allowed us to cool Cs atoms to ~5 µK in a 2 mK deep tweezer and image them with a suppressed photon background.
Future directions · Chapter 7
Toward ultracold Li–Cs mixtures
The longer-term aim of the lab is to trap Li and Cs atoms together in a shared tweezer, perform Feshbach spectroscopy to map out their interspecies interactions, and eventually form ground-state LiCs molecules — which have a large permanent electric dipole moment (~5.5 Debye). The large mass imbalance between the two species also makes sympathetic cooling worth exploring, alongside 3D extensions of the cooling simulations developed in this thesis.

Why this matters beyond the lab. Optical tweezer arrays are one of the leading platforms for neutral-atom quantum computing — companies including QuEra, Atom Computing, and Infleqtion are building processors along these lines. Two of the main technical bottlenecks are imaging fidelity (how reliably you can read out qubit states without losing the atom) and qubit temperature (a warmer atom accumulates more errors during two-qubit gates). The Li and Cs work in this thesis addresses both of those directly. That said, the path to fault-tolerant quantum computing is genuinely long, and the contribution here is at the level of improving individual components — not solving the problem.

Methods & Tools

Technical approaches used in this work

The thesis involves a mix of experimental hardware, laser physics, and computational tools. A few worth mentioning:

Spatial Light Modulators (SLMs)
Used to generate vortex beams with controlled orbital angular momentum for background-free Cs imaging. The SLM lets us shape the transverse phase profile of the 685 nm beam in real time.
Gaussian Process Optimization
Experimental parameters (trap depth, laser detuning, pulse durations) were optimized using Gaussian process regression, which makes efficient use of noisy measurements to find good operating points.
QuTiP Simulations
The cooling theory was validated with open quantum systems simulations using QuTiP (Python), solving the Lindblad master equation for a bound atom with spin in the Lamb-Dicke regime.
Laser Frequency Stabilization
Multiple laser systems (671 nm, 685 nm, 852 nm, 1064 nm) are stabilized using saturated-absorption spectroscopy, beat-note locking, and Pound-Drever-Hall cavity locking in a hierarchy suited to each application.
Magic-Ellipticity Polarization
For Cs sideband cooling, a specific trap polarization was tuned to equalize the AC Stark shifts of the ground and excited states, suppressing differential light shifts that would otherwise broaden the cooling transition.
